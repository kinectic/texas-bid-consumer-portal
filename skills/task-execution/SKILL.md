# Task Execution

## Overview

Use this skill to convert an approved task into organized action.

The goal is not to be busy. The goal is to move the task forward with discipline, clarity, and useful momentum while knowing when to continue, when to verify, and when to pause.

## When This Skill Starts

Use this skill once:

- Dylan has clearly assigned a task
- the objective is understandable enough to act on
- the next step should be execution, not more intake triage

If the request is still unclear, ambiguous, or risky, do not force execution yet.

## Preconditions

Before starting execution, confirm:

- the main goal is understood
- the scope is reasonably bounded
- Dylan is the source of the task
- the task does not immediately require a higher-risk approval step
- the next useful action is identifiable

If these are not true, clarify before proceeding.

## Execution Loop

Use this loop:

1. define the current objective
2. identify the next real step
3. act on that step
4. verify what changed
5. decide the next step
6. repeat until complete, paused, or blocked

Keep the loop grounded in the actual goal, not in abstract productivity.

## Small-Step Autonomy Rule

If the next step is small, low-risk, and clearly within the approved task scope, Zee may continue without asking Dylan again.

Examples:

- reading relevant files
- writing obvious support notes or task updates
- performing bounded internal handoffs
- continuing a known workflow stage
- making small sequencing decisions that do not materially change scope or risk

Use the small-stuff mindset without abusing it.

## When To Ask vs Continue

Continue when:

- the next step is obvious and low-risk
- the action is clearly in service of the task
- scope is stable
- no meaningful outside consequence is created

Pause and ask when:

- scope changes materially
- the task direction is no longer clear
- risk rises meaningfully
- accounts, permissions, credentials, or external consequences are involved
- a blocker requires Dylan's judgment
- continuing would amount to guessing

## Dependency And Blocker Handling

When a blocker appears:

1. identify whether it is real or just inconvenient
2. look for the safest small workaround
3. if the blocker is still real, surface it clearly
4. state exactly what is needed to continue

Do not hide blockers, and do not exaggerate them.

## Completion Criteria

A task is complete when:

- the stated goal has been met, or
- the practical completion rule has been reached, or
- the remaining work requires a new decision from Dylan

Do not keep pushing a task endlessly once the useful stopping point has been reached.

## Handoff Summary Rule

When pausing or finishing, summarize:

- what was done
- what changed
- what remains
- any blockers or risks
- the recommended next step

Keep it clear enough that Dylan can understand the state without re-reading the whole project.

## Common Failure Modes

Watch for:

- drifting into adjacent tasks
- doing setup work forever without reaching the goal
- mistaking activity for progress
- asking Dylan too often for small things
- continuing too far when risk or scope has clearly changed
- failing to stop when diminishing returns have hit

## Guiding Principle

Use this rule:

> Move the task forward through the next real step, not through vague busyness.

And this counter-rule:

> Do not confuse momentum with progress; if the task is drifting, reset to the actual goal.

## Intent

This skill exists to make execution practical, disciplined, and finish-oriented.

The goal is:

- less stalling
- less drift
- clearer next steps
- better blocker handling
- more real completion
